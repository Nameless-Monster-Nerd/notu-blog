🔊 How Speakers Work: The Hidden Science of Turning Electricity into Sound

Subject: Physical Science (NGSS PS4.B — Electromagnetic Radiation, PS4.A — Wave Properties) | Grade Level: 6–8 | Reading Time: 10 minutes

Put your hand on a speaker the next time you play music. Feel that? It's vibrating. Not just a little buzz — the whole cone is moving back and forth, pushing air molecules around to create the sound you hear. But here's the question that doesn't get asked enough: how does a tiny electrical signal from your phone turn into a full drum beat or a singer's voice? The answer involves electromagnetism, a coil of wire, and a magnet — and it's one of the coolest examples of physics in action, happening inside every speaker, every headphone, every earbud you own.

It Starts with a Simple Circuit

A speaker is basically a circuit — a complete loop that electricity flows through. When your phone or computer sends a music signal, it's sending an electrical current that changes direction hundreds or thousands of times per second. That alternating current (AC) travels through wires inside the speaker and reaches a coil of wire wrapped around a tube, called the voice coil.

⚡ Alternating Current (AC) ⚡ Voice Coil ⚡ Electromagnet ⚡ Diaphragm/Cone ⚡ Magnetic Field

Now here's the key: when electricity flows through a coil of wire, it creates a magnetic field around it. The more current, the stronger the field. And when that coil is placed right next to a permanent magnet (the big ring magnet you can see if you pop open an old speaker), the two magnetic fields push and pull against each other. The coil moves back and forth — and since it's attached to the speaker cone (the paper or plastic part you see), the cone moves too.

From Signal to Sound, Step by Step

  1. Your phone plays a song. The digital music file is converted to an electrical signal — a voltage that changes shape to match the sound wave.
  2. The amplifier boosts the signal. The tiny voltage from your phone isn't strong enough to move a speaker. The amplifier increases it to a level that can drive the voice coil.
  3. The voice coil becomes an electromagnet. The alternating current creates a magnetic field that flips direction every time the audio wave crosses zero.
  4. Push and pull. The voice coil's magnetic field interacts with the permanent magnet. Like poles repel, opposites attract — so the coil vibrates back and forth at the same frequency as the audio signal.
  5. The cone moves air. That vibration pushes the cone, which compresses and rarefies the air in front of it, creating sound waves.
  6. Your ears detect the waves. The sound waves travel through the air, hit your eardrum, and your brain interprets them as music.

Microphones: The Reverse of a Speaker

Here's the mind-blowing part: a microphone works backwards from a speaker. Instead of turning electricity into sound, it turns sound into electricity.

In a dynamic microphone (the kind you see singers holding on stage), sound waves hit a thin diaphragm that's attached to a coil of wire, suspended inside a magnetic field. When the sound waves push the diaphragm, the coil moves within the magnetic field, which generates a small electrical current. This is called electromagnetic induction — the same principle that powers electrical generators in power plants. A whisper makes a tiny current. A shout makes a bigger one. That current travels down the microphone cable, through the mixing board, and eventually — hundreds of thousands of times per second — reproduces the original sound with stunning accuracy.

🔬 Try This: The Speaker-as-Microphone Experiment

You can actually turn a speaker into a microphone — and it proves the reverse principle perfectly.

  1. Find an old, small speaker (like a computer speaker or a headphone driver).
  2. Connect the two wires (or leads) to the input of a recording device — a phone with a microphone input adapter, or a simple audio interface.
  3. Tap gently on the speaker cone. Do you see the recording level meter move?
  4. Now speak into the cone. Can you hear your voice through the recording?
  5. Try tapping with different force — the signal should change just like a real microphone.

🧪 Experiment: Build a Simple Speaker (NGSS PS4.B)

Materials: Small paper cup, insulated copper wire (22–28 gauge, about 3 meters), a strong neodymium disc magnet, scissors, a 3.5mm audio cable (one you can cut or an old pair of headphones), a phone or music player

  1. Wind the copper wire into a tight coil about the size of a quarter — leave 20cm of wire free on each end.
  2. Sand the enamel coating off the ends of the wire (just the last 2cm) so the copper is exposed.
  3. Attach the coil to the bottom of the paper cup with tape — centered and flat.
  4. Place the neodymium magnet on top of the coil (it should stick to the coil's core).
  5. Strip the ends of the audio cable and connect one wire to each end of the coil.
  6. Plug the other end into your phone and play music. The cup should vibrate and produce sound!

Prediction question: What happens if you use a stronger magnet? What happens if you remove the cup? Why do you think the cup is important?
