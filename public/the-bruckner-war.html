The 3-Month War Against Bruckner Blvd and LaGuardia

#Soundproofing#Bronx#Bruckner#LaGuardia#StudioBuild#RoomWithinRoom

You know what nobody tells you about soundproofing a shed-turned-studio at 1389 Kearney Ave?

The planes.

Bruckner Boulevard traffic was bad enough — 18-wheelers shaking the ground every 30 seconds, motorcycles treating the boulevard like a racetrack at 2 AM. I planned for that. Rockwool, mass-loaded vinyl, double drywall with Green Glue. That's a tank. That handles traffic.

But LaGuardia? Man.

I didn't realize until after we framed the first wall. Standing there, measuring for the room-within-a-room gap, and suddenly the whole structure starts vibrating. Not like a truck — like someone grabbed the building by the corners and shook.

Looked up. A plane was maybe 1,500 feet above, lined up straight with runway 13/31. When you look at a map, Kearney Ave sits right under the final approach path. Those planes aren't circling — they're coming in hot, engines screaming, gear down, straight over your head.

The first month was pure denial. "We'll just add more insulation." "Seal the gaps better." "Maybe it won't be that bad."

It was that bad.

The shed itself was the problem. It wasn't built for this. It was a shade structure — thin walls, single-ply roof, no foundation to speak of. Every plane that passed turned the whole thing into a speaker cabinet. The corrugated metal roof would resonate at specific frequencies (later I'd learn those were around 85-110 Hz — right in the range of jet engine rumble). The whole room was a drum.

Month two was when we got serious. Demo'd everything down to the frame. Started fresh with a room-within-a-room design.

Step one: The floating floor

Concrete slab was conducting vibration straight up through the structure. We laid down cork underlayment, then OSB, then mass-loaded vinyl, then another layer of OSB. The floor stopped being a drum head and started being a sandwich — mass, decoupling, mass.

Step two: The walls

Existing structure got one layer of 5/8" drywall with Green Glue. Then we built a second frame inside it — completely independent, touching nothing but the floated floor. No hard contact with the original walls anywhere. That gap between the two frames? That's the airlock. That's where the sound dies.

Step three: The nightmare — the roof

A plane flies 1,500 feet overhead. That's not sound traveling through air — that's sound pressing on the structure like a hand on a drum skin. We ended up with: original metal roof → insulation → new OSB sheathing → Green Glue → second layer OSB → mass-loaded vinyl → air gap → acoustic ceiling tile. Seven layers.

Step four: The door

Solid core, drop seal at the bottom, magnetic weatherstripping on all four sides. That door alone cost more than the first month's worth of insulation.

Month three was testing. Put a speaker inside playing pink noise at 90 dB. Walked outside. Nothing. Put a Bluetooth speaker on the Bruckner sidewalk playing traffic noise at full volume. Went inside. Barely a whisper.

Then the real test — sitting in silence during a LaGuardia landing.

The plane came. The walls vibrated slightly. I could feel the pressure change in the room. But my ears? Nothing. Not a rumble, not a drone. Just... quiet.

Three months. A shed that wanted to be a drum. A highway that never sleeps. A flight path that rattles your teeth. And a room that finally, finally sounds like nothing at all.

That's the silence you pay for. Not the absence of noise — the silence you earned.
