🎵 The Science of Sound Waves

Subject: Physical Science (NGSS PS4.A) | Grade Level: 6–8 | Reading Time: 12 minutes

Think about your favorite song. Maybe it's something you heard on the 6 train this morning. Maybe it's a track you recorded at a local studio. However you heard it, one thing is true: sound made it happen. But how? What is sound, really?

Sound is energy in motion. It travels as a wave through air, water, or even solids — and the science behind it is cooler than you think.

What Is a Sound Wave?

When something vibrates — a guitar string, a speaker cone, your vocal cords — it pushes against nearby air molecules. Those molecules bump into the next ones, and the next ones, like a domino effect that never stops. That chain of vibrations is a sound wave.

Sound waves need a medium to travel through. That's why space is silent — no air means no sound. But here on Earth, sound moves through solids faster than liquids, and liquids faster than gases. If you put your ear to a train track, you can hear an approaching train long before you hear it through the air. Sound travels through steel at about 5,960 meters per second — 17 times faster than through air!

🧊 Bronx Connection: The 2/5 subway lines run on steel tracks. Next time you're waiting at the 149th Street station, notice how you can feel the train coming before you hear it. That's sound waves traveling through steel and concrete faster than they can reach you through the air. Physics is happening all around you.

Frequency: How Fast the Wave Moves

A wave's frequency is how many times it vibrates per second. We measure this in hertz (Hz). One hertz = one vibration per second.

  • Low frequency (like a bass drum or a subway rumble) = fewer vibrations per second. Deep, low sound. This is what you feel in your chest at a concert.
  • High frequency (like a whistle or a cymbal) = more vibrations per second. High, sharp sound.

Humans can hear between about 20 Hz (a very deep rumble) and 20,000 Hz (a very high-pitched squeak). Dogs can hear up to 45,000 Hz — that's why dog whistles work. And bats? They can hear up to 200,000 Hz. That's called ultrasound.

🔬 Try This: Find Your Frequency Range

You'll need a computer or phone with a frequency tone generator app (search "frequency generator" — many are free). With a friend or adult nearby:

  1. Start with a low tone at 100 Hz. Can you hear it? It should sound like a deep hum.
  2. Slowly increase the frequency. Notice how the pitch gets higher.
  3. Keep going until you can't hear the sound anymore. Write down the highest frequency you can detect.
  4. Compare with a friend — everyone's range is different!

Amplitude: How Loud the Wave Is

Amplitude is the wave's height — the distance from the wave's resting position to its peak. Bigger amplitude = more energy = louder sound.

We measure loudness in decibels (dB):

SoundDecibels (dB)What It Feels Like
A whisper30 dBVery quiet
Normal conversation60 dBComfortable
City traffic from inside a car80 dBNoticeable
Subway train arriving at platform95 dBLoud — can damage hearing with long exposure
Rock concert / Car horn nearby110 dBPainful — can cause hearing damage in minutes
Jet engine at takeoff140 dBPain threshold

Here's the wild part: every 10 dB increase makes the sound 10 times more intense! So a 90 dB sound isn't just a little louder than 80 dB — it's 10 times more powerful.

How Your Brain Turns Waves Into Music

Your outer ear (the part you can see) funnels sound into your ear canal. The sound wave hits your eardrum — a thin membrane that vibrates like a drum skin. Those vibrations pass through three tiny bones (the smallest bones in your body — the hammer, anvil, and stirrup) into your inner ear, where fluid and tiny hair cells convert them into electrical signals.

Your brain receives those electrical signals and interprets them. That's how you hear a friend's voice, a subway announcement, or a 528 Hz healing frequency playing through studio speakers in a soundproof room.

Real-World Application: Soundproofing Your World

This is where science meets real life. Soundproofing works by stopping sound waves from traveling from one space to another. Here's how:

  • Mass: Thick, heavy materials (like concrete or dense drywall) make it harder for vibrations to pass through.
  • Decoupling: Separating walls so vibrations don't travel from one side to the other.
  • Absorption: Soft materials (foam, carpet, mattresses) absorb sound energy instead of reflecting it.
  • Sealing: Every crack or gap is a highway for sound waves. Sealing them blocks the path.
🏠 Lab Life: At Mohikontok Sound Lab (1389 Kearney Ave, Bronx), the walls had to block traffic noise from the Bruckner Expressway. The solution? Multiple layers of mass-loaded vinyl, decoupled wall frames, and carefully sealed seams. Sound waves don't stand a chance — and inside the room, you can hear your own heartbeat.

🧪 CLASSROOM ACTIVITY: Make a Soundproof Box

NGSS PS4.A: Use evidence to construct an explanation about how sound waves transfer energy.

Materials: Shoebox, smartphone with alarm/timer, various materials (cotton balls, aluminum foil, bubble wrap, towels, plastic wrap, tape)

Instructions:

  1. Place your phone in the box with the alarm set to play for 10 seconds.
  2. Predict: Which material will block the most sound?
  3. Test each material by wrapping or layering it around/over the box. Hold the box at arm's length and rate how loud the alarm sounds on a scale of 1–5.
  4. Record your results: which material reduced sound the most? The least?
  5. Explain: Why do some materials absorb sound while others let it pass?

Frequency in Action: Healing Tones

Scientists and musicians have explored how different frequencies affect the human body and mind. This is called sound therapy or frequency healing.

  • 174 Hz: Low, grounding — like a deep breath for your nervous system
  • 396 Hz: Releases tension and fear — often described as "letting go" frequency
  • 528 Hz: Known as the "love frequency" — associated with transformation and emotional balance
  • 639 Hz: Helps with connection and communication in relationships
  • 741 Hz: Clears the mind for problem-solving and creative expression
  • 852 Hz: Awakens intuition and inner peace

When you play one of these frequencies in a soundproof room — where no outside noise interferes — your brain can fully absorb the vibration. Some studies suggest these frequencies can reduce anxiety, improve focus, and even support healing. The science is still emerging, but the results from people who use them are powerful.
